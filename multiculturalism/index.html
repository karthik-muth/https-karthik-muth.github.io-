A visual history of "multiculturalism" in Sweden - and the society's attitude towards it




Sweden is one of the most multicultural countries in Europe today, with more than a third of its population represented by first- and second-generation immigrants.

This can be traced back to a decision made in 1975 by the Swedish parliament - and the prime minister at the time, Olof Palme - to make the country a pluralistic society in terms of ethnicities, culture and religion.

A brief look until 1970

Before the second world war, the social democrat concept of Folkhemmet (the people’s homes) aimed to modernize society while diminishing the impact of social class. Assimilation, as opposed to integration, was the political stance regarding immigrants. Sweden was largely culturally and linguistically homogeneous at this time except for the Sami and Tornedalian minorities.

After the war, Sweden opened its borders primarily for labour immigration.

In the 1950s and 1960s, migrant workers moved from Finland and other European countries. By the end of 1970, the share of the foreign-born population was under 7%, and nearly all of them were from Europe.

On 14 May 1975, the parliament led by the Social Democrat government of Olof Palme voted in favour of a new immigrant and minority policy. Sweden, along with Canada and Australia “became one of the first countries in the West to adopt the idea of multiculturalism formally”.

Sweden started accepting refugees and people came from the Middle East, South America and Yugoslavia due to wars in their countries. In 2000, more than 11% of the population was represented by people born abroad.

Sweden joined the European Union In 1995 and the Schengen area in 2001. Following this, Sweden more immigration from Europe. By the end of 2010, nearly 15% of the population were foreign-born and around 6% were from outside Europe.

The 2010s saw more than 100k immigrants moving to Sweden each year reaching the peak of 163k in 2016 during the Syrian civil war. By the end of 2021, nearly 20% of the population was represented by people born abroad.

By the end of 2021, the population of Sweden was around 10.45 million of which around 2.1 million (20%) are foreign-born. They represent more than 150 nationalities, making Sweden one of the most diverse countries in the world.

In addition to the foreign-born people, 6% of the population are born in Sweden to two foreign-born parents. Thus more than a quarter of the Swedish population are of foreign background.
